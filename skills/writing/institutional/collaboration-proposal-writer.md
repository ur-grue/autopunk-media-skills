# Collaboration Proposal Writer

## What This Skill Does
Writes a formal collaboration proposal addressed to another media outlet, broadcaster, production company, or independent producer, making the case for a specific joint project and structuring a clear offer of terms.

## When To Use This Skill
- You want to approach another organisation about co-producing, co-publishing, or co-distributing a specific piece of work
- You have identified a potential partner with an audience, capability, or resource that complements your own, and need to make a written pitch
- You are proposing a content-sharing arrangement, joint investigation, or co-commission and need a document that can go to an editor, head of department, or executive producer
- You have had an exploratory conversation with a potential partner and need to follow up with something concrete and professional

## What You Need To Provide
**Required:**
- Your organisation's name and a one-line description of what you do (or a description of yourself if you are an independent)
- The potential partner's name and why you have chosen them specifically — what they bring that you cannot supply alone
- The project: what it is, what format it would take, and what you need to produce it
- What you are proposing to contribute (reporting, funding, distribution, production capacity, archive access, or some combination)
- What you are asking the partner to contribute

**Optional:**
- The editorial angle or central question of the project
- Any relevant market or audience data that supports the case for the project
- Timeline or publication window you have in mind
- Whether there is exclusivity on the offer, or whether you are approaching multiple potential partners
- Preferred tone: formal letter, informal pitch memo, or structured proposal document

## How Claude Approaches This
1. Opens by establishing why this specific partner is being approached — grounding the proposal in the partner's work, audience, or capability rather than generic flattery
2. Introduces the project concisely: the subject, why it matters now, and what it would produce
3. Lays out the proposed division of contribution — what each party brings — in terms that are specific enough to be taken seriously but not so detailed that they foreclose negotiation
4. Articulates the shared benefit: what both organisations gain from the collaboration rather than focusing only on what you need
5. Closes with a clear next step — a call, a meeting, or a request for an indication of interest — and a realistic timeline

## Output Format
A structured proposal document of 400–600 words, or a shorter pitch memo of 200–300 words if you request it. Formal professional register throughout. Written in the first person (your voice) or in institutional third person (our organisation's voice) — specify which. Structured with short paragraphs and, optionally, a brief bullet-point breakdown of contribution terms. The tone is confident and collegial: one professional addressing another, not a supplicant asking a favour.

## Quality Criteria
- [ ] The opening makes clear why this specific partner has been chosen — no generic "we admire your work" without content
- [ ] The project is described precisely enough that the recipient can form a clear picture without further research
- [ ] The contribution ask is concrete — what exactly is being requested, not just "resources" or "support"
- [ ] The benefit to the partner is stated explicitly, not assumed
- [ ] The close proposes a specific next step with a timeframe
- [ ] The tone is appropriate for a first formal approach — direct without being demanding, warm without being informal

## Example

### Input
**My organisation:** A regional investigative newsroom covering the north of the country, with a strong track record in data journalism and freedom of information reporting, and an audience of around 80,000 monthly readers.

**Potential partner:** A national public radio broadcaster with a weekly documentary strand that covers domestic affairs.

**Why this partner:** Their documentary strand reaches an audience we don't — audio listeners who are not digital news readers — and they have production infrastructure we lack.

**The project:** A joint investigation into the use of temporary accommodation contracts by local authorities. We have obtained a dataset through FOI covering five years of contracts and spending in our region. We want to expand the scope nationally and produce both a written investigation and an audio documentary.

**What we bring:** The dataset, the FOI methodology, three months of existing reporting, and the written publication.

**What we are asking for:** Co-reporting support to gather equivalent data nationally, audio production capacity, and broadcast distribution through their documentary strand.

**Timeline:** We would like to publish in six months, timed to coincide with the local authority budget cycle.

### Output
**TO:** Documentary Commissioning, [Broadcaster]
**FROM:** [Investigative Newsroom]
**RE:** Collaboration proposal — temporary accommodation investigation
**DATE:** March 2026

We are writing to propose a collaborative investigation between our newsroom and your documentary strand, combining our existing data work with your national reporting capacity and audio production expertise.

Over the past three months, we have built what we believe is the most detailed dataset yet compiled on temporary accommodation contracts issued by local authorities in our region — five years of spending records, contract lengths, and provider relationships, obtained through a series of freedom of information requests. The picture it reveals is troubling: a pattern of short-term contracts at above-market rates, concentrated among a small number of providers, that appears to have deepened rather than resolved the housing pressures it was designed to address.

The story is regional for now. We want to make it national.

Your documentary strand's track record in domestic affairs, combined with your reach among audio audiences who do not read digital news, makes you the partner best placed to help us do that. We are proposing a collaboration structured as follows: we contribute the existing dataset, the FOI methodology, and three months of reporting, and we publish the written investigation. You contribute co-reporting support to gather equivalent data in other regions, audio production capacity, and broadcast distribution through the documentary strand. All editorial decisions would be made jointly.

The timing matters. Local authority budgets are set in September. A publication in early August would land the investigation when decision-makers are actively engaged with spending choices — and when public interest in housing costs is consistently high.

We are not approaching other broadcasters with this proposal at this stage. We would welcome a conversation in the next two weeks to discuss whether there is appetite to develop this further.

Please feel free to contact us at any point.

## Known Limitations
- This skill writes the opening proposal document, not a full co-production agreement or term sheet; if the approach is successful, legal and financial terms will need separate professional handling
- The proposal will be most effective when the contribution split is genuinely mutual; if the ask is heavily weighted toward one party, Claude will flag this but cannot resolve an underlying imbalance in the deal
- For proposals involving international co-productions or broadcast rights across territories, the document produced here is a starting point only — rights and regulatory considerations will need specialist input
- If you do not know enough about the potential partner's current editorial priorities, the proposal may misjudge what they are likely to find attractive; some pre-approach research will always improve results
