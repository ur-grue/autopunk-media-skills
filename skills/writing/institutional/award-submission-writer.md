# Award Submission Writer

## What This Skill Does
Writes a compelling award submission entry that frames a piece of journalism or media work within a specific award category's criteria, highlighting impact, craft, and originality.

## When To Use This Skill
- You have completed a piece of work — a documentary, investigative report, feature, podcast series, or video — and want to enter it for an industry award
- An award deadline is approaching and you need a submission statement, supporting narrative, or category entry form text
- You want to translate the internal story of how a piece was made into the persuasive language award panels respond to
- You are submitting across multiple categories and need to angle the same work differently for each

## What You Need To Provide
**Required:**
- Title and format of the work (e.g., "six-part documentary series," "longform magazine investigation," "radio documentary")
- The award category you are entering (e.g., "Best Investigative Journalism," "Best New Documentary Talent," "Excellence in Science Reporting")
- A summary of what the work covers — the subject, the central question it answers, and what you found or showed
- The specific impact or outcome: audience figures, policy response, public reaction, critical reception, or any measurable result

**Optional:**
- Any unusual production circumstances (access challenges, safety considerations, tight timeline)
- Named contributors whose work you want to highlight (reporter, director, producer, editor)
- Word count or character limit for the submission
- Tone guidance: formal and restrained, or passionate and personal

## How Claude Approaches This
1. Reads the award category criteria closely (as described by you) and identifies which qualities the judging panel will weight most heavily — originality, public interest, craft, or demonstrable impact
2. Opens with the strongest possible hook: the central revelation, the human story at the heart of the work, or the obstacle overcome — whichever best fits the category framing
3. Builds the body of the submission in three movements: what the work set out to do, how it was done (process and craft), and what it achieved — anchoring each claim to the specific evidence you provided
4. Closes with a direct statement of why this work belongs in this category, written in the confident but not boastful register that award panels respond to

## Output Format
A single continuous submission text, 300–500 words unless you specify a different limit. Paragraphs of 3–5 sentences. Formal but human register — the voice of a confident professional addressing peers, not a marketing pitch. No bullet points. No headers. Plain prose throughout. If you request a shorter version (e.g., 150-word entry form text), Claude will produce a tighter version that preserves the strongest claims.

## Quality Criteria
- [ ] The opening sentence is specific to the work — no generic framing like "In a world where journalism matters..."
- [ ] Every claim about impact is tied to a concrete detail provided in the input (no invented statistics or vague assertions)
- [ ] The submission addresses the specific award category rather than being generic praise of the work
- [ ] The tone is confident without crossing into self-congratulation
- [ ] Production craft is acknowledged without becoming technical jargon inaccessible to a non-specialist judge
- [ ] The closing sentence gives the panel a clear reason to choose this entry

## Example

### Input
**Work:** A four-part documentary series examining how a mid-size city's public housing authority failed its residents over two decades, through a combination of deferred maintenance, financial mismanagement, and political indifference.

**Award category:** Best Investigative Documentary Series

**What we found:** Internal maintenance logs obtained through freedom of information requests showed that 74% of urgent repair orders in one district were closed without any work being done. We interviewed 43 current and former residents, 12 former housing authority employees, and three city councillors who had oversight responsibility during this period.

**Impact:** Following broadcast, the city council voted to commission an independent audit of the housing authority. Two senior officials resigned. The series won a strong audience — averaging 1.1 million viewers across the four episodes on first broadcast — and generated sustained coverage in the national press.

**Contributors to highlight:** Lead investigative reporter, archive researcher who identified the maintenance log pattern, and the two residents who agreed to appear on camera across all four episodes.

### Output
For two years, residents of a public housing district in one of the country's largest cities had been told their repair requests were being handled. The maintenance logs told a different story: 74% of urgent orders in the area had been marked closed without any work done. That gap between official assurance and documented reality became the spine of this four-part investigative series — and the starting point for one of the most consequential pieces of accountability journalism in our outlet's recent history.

The series began not with documents but with people. Over eighteen months, the reporting team conducted 43 interviews with current and former residents, building the kind of trust that allowed two families to appear across all four episodes, sharing their experiences on the record in circumstances that carried real personal risk. Those relationships gave the series its emotional architecture. The freedom of information work — led by an archive researcher who identified the maintenance log pattern after months of requests and appeals — gave it its evidentiary foundation.

The result was journalism that held a system to account across four hours of primetime documentary television. An average audience of 1.1 million viewers per episode demonstrated the scale of public interest in a story that had been ignored for two decades. The response from institutions was swift: the city council voted to commission an independent audit of the housing authority within three weeks of the final episode, and two senior officials resigned before that audit concluded.

This series belongs in the Best Investigative Documentary category because it demonstrates what the form can do at its best: patient reporting, rigorous documentation, and a commitment to the people most affected, combined in a way that produced consequences that outlasted the broadcast. It is the kind of work this award was created to recognise.

## Known Limitations
- If you cannot provide concrete impact figures — audience numbers, policy responses, or critical reception — the submission will be noticeably weaker; Claude cannot invent credible impact claims and will flag where gaps exist
- Award panels vary enormously in culture and preference; a submission drafted for a formal industry body may need significant tonal adjustment for a peer-voted or audience-facing award
- This skill writes submission text, not supporting materials — if the entry requires a separate production statement, director's note, or technical breakdown, those need to be requested as separate tasks
- For very short entry forms (under 100 words), important nuance will inevitably be lost; Claude will prioritise impact and category fit but cannot preserve everything
