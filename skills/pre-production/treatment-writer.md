# Treatment Writer

## What This Skill Does
Writes a short-form production treatment (2–4 pages) for pitching a documentary or news feature — a narrative document that tells the story of the film before a frame is shot, giving commissioners and executives enough to say yes.

## When To Use This Skill
- You have a documentary or long-form news feature idea and need a treatment to send to a commissioning editor, broadcaster, or streaming platform
- You are preparing a pitch meeting and need a written document that stands alone without you in the room to explain it
- You have rough notes, research, or a pitch deck and need to convert them into a flowing narrative document
- You are applying for development funding and the application requires a written treatment

## What You Need To Provide
**Required:** The core story — what happens, who the central characters or subjects are, and what the film argues or reveals. The proposed format (single documentary, series, news feature, podcast). The target length or runtime.
**Optional:** Target broadcaster, platform, or outlet; tone references (other films or programmes with a similar feel); confirmed access or contributors; proposed visual approach; any financing already in place.

## How Claude Approaches This
1. Opens with a cinematic or narrative hook — a scene, a moment, or a question that draws the reader in immediately and establishes the film's emotional register.
2. Introduces the central story and its characters in the order a viewer would encounter them, written in present tense as if the film is unfolding in real time.
3. Builds the narrative arc through the body of the treatment: what is at stake, how the story develops, what complications or revelations emerge, how it resolves (or intentionally does not).
4. Closes with a brief section on format, tone, and approach — the "filmmaker's statement" equivalent — explaining why this story must be told now, what unique access or perspective the production has, and what the audience will take away.

## Output Format
2–4 pages (600–1,200 words). Written in present tense, third-person narrative prose — not bullet points, not a synopsis. Reads like the beginning of the film itself. Sections are separated by short bold subheadings if needed for clarity. Ends with a paragraph on format, tone, and the filmmakers' access or angle. Language is vivid, precise, and professional — the same register as a treatment submitted to a major broadcaster.

## Quality Criteria
- [ ] Opens with a specific scene, image, or moment — not a general statement about the subject
- [ ] Central character or subject is introduced with enough specificity to feel real within the first paragraph
- [ ] The story has a visible arc: a beginning situation, a development or complication, and a destination (even if unresolved)
- [ ] The treatment explains why this story is being told now — the news hook, the access, the timeliness
- [ ] Tone is consistent throughout — does not shift between journalistic and promotional registers
- [ ] The closing paragraph addresses format, runtime, and what the audience will gain

## Example

### Input
Story: A community of fisherpeople on a remote island chain has been fishing the same waters for generations. Over the past five years, industrial trawlers operating under licences issued by the national government have stripped the area, and the local catch has collapsed by 80%. The community is now taking the government to court in a landmark case that will determine whether traditional fishing rights can override commercial licences. Central characters: an elderly fisherman who has fished these waters for 50 years and is the lead plaintiff; his granddaughter, a lawyer who left the city to take the case; and the government minister who issued the licences. Format: single 75-minute documentary. Target: public broadcaster.

### Output

**Treatment: The Last Catch**
*A documentary feature — 75 minutes*

---

**Opening**

Before dawn, Marcelino Ferrão has already been on the water for two hours. He is seventy-one years old. His father taught him these channels; his grandfather taught his father. He knows where the yellowfin run in autumn and where the sea floor drops away to nothing forty metres below the surface. He knows the water the way a farmer knows his land.

This morning, the net comes up almost empty.

---

**The Story**

The Ferrão family has fished the Ilha Longa archipelago for five generations. So have the Costas, the Mendes, the Brites. The seventeen islands that make up this remote chain, three hours by ferry from the mainland, have sustained roughly 400 families for as long as anyone can remember. The fish were always there. Until they weren't.

In 2019, the national government issued a series of commercial fishing licences to three industrial trawler companies — licences that cover the same waters the island communities have worked for generations. The trawlers arrived with nets wide enough to sweep the sea floor clean. In five years, the recorded catch on Ilha Longa has fallen by 80 per cent. The community's income has collapsed. Young people are leaving.

Marcelino Ferrão is not leaving. And he is not giving up.

Six months ago, with the help of his granddaughter — Beatriz Ferrão, 34, a maritime lawyer who grew up watching her grandfather go out before dawn every morning — Marcelino became the lead plaintiff in a case that has no precedent in this country's courts. The claim is straightforward: that the government's commercial licences violated customary fishing rights that predate the nation's constitution. The legal argument is anything but simple.

**The Characters**

Marcelino is a precise, unhurried man who speaks about the sea with the authority of someone who has been listening to it for seven decades. He is not angry in the way that makes for easy television. He is something more difficult and more interesting: certain. He knows what happened to the fish. He knows who is responsible. He is prepared to wait for the courts to agree.

Beatriz left the city to take this case. She has a daughter, a mortgage, and a career that her partners at the firm regard as temporarily interrupted. She is under no illusions about the odds. Maritime customary rights cases have failed in neighbouring jurisdictions. The precedents are against her. She is pursuing this anyway, because she grew up on this island and because she cannot look at her grandfather's empty nets and call it someone else's problem.

The government minister who signed the commercial licences, Estado Oliveira, is a careful politician who has agreed, through his office, to appear in the film. He argues that the licences were issued legally, that the trawler companies employ several hundred people on the mainland, and that the correlation between commercial fishing and the catch decline is not proven. He is not a villain. He made a decision that the law permitted him to make. The film will ask him, on camera, whether he regrets it.

**The Arc**

The court case is the spine of the film. We will follow it from the first hearing through to its conclusion — whatever that conclusion is. The filmmakers have negotiated access to both the plaintiffs and their legal team throughout the proceedings. We will be present for the preparation, the hearings, the setbacks, and the verdict.

But the court is not the film's heart. The film's heart is the question of what Marcelino Ferrão does on the morning after a hearing goes badly. Does he go out anyway? What does it mean to continue working the sea when the sea has been emptied? What does a community do when the thing that made it a community no longer exists in sufficient quantity to sustain it?

**Approach**

*The Last Catch* is an observational documentary. There is no narration. The argument is made through accumulated detail: the weight of the empty nets, the silence in the dockside market, the drive to the capital for a hearing in a building that has never seen the archipelago and cannot picture it.

The film is made possible by a level of access that took eighteen months to establish. Marcelino Ferrão has never spoken to a camera before. The legal team have agreed to allow filming inside their preparation sessions. The minister's agreement to participate is, in itself, a development: his office declined two previous documentary inquiries.

This is a film about fish, about law, and about what one family is willing to do to be heard. It is also a film about what happens when the state and the sea reach different conclusions about who owns the water.

---

*Runtime: 75 minutes. Proposed broadcast window: autumn. Production company holds full access agreements with all principal subjects.*

## Known Limitations
- A treatment is a persuasion document as much as a story document. Claude can write a compelling treatment from strong material, but cannot manufacture narrative tension or character depth that the underlying story does not contain. Thin or vague story ideas produce serviceable but uninspiring treatments.
- Treatments written for specific broadcasters (BBC, Channel 4, Netflix) benefit from matching the house style and commissioning priorities of that outlet. Claude can approximate this if the target broadcaster is specified, but manual adjustment by someone familiar with that outlet's current slate is advisable before submission.
- If the story is unresolved or the ending is genuinely unknown (as with ongoing legal cases or investigations), Claude will write the treatment to reflect that uncertainty honestly — which is correct practice but may require additional framing for commissioners who want a cleaner narrative arc.
