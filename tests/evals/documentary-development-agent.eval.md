## Test Case 2: STILL CONNECTED (series, streaming platform)

### Brief

A four-part documentary series (4 x 52 minutes) about long-distance relationships maintained across conflict zones. Each episode follows a different couple: a Syrian refugee in Berlin and her husband still in Idlib; a Ukrainian soldier and his wife who evacuated to Portugal; a Somali journalist in Nairobi and her mother in Mogadishu; a Venezuelan nurse in Bogota and her teenage daughter who stayed in Caracas. The series examines how love, parenting, and daily intimacy survive when sustained entirely through phone screens. Targeting Netflix or Apple TV+ as the primary buyer. The tone is intimate and emotionally direct — think "Midnight Family" meets the visual language of "Searching." Some footage has already been gathered via screen-recorded video calls. The director has relationships with three of the four families.

### Simulated deliverable

**Pitch Treatment**

**Logline**
Four couples separated by conflict keep their relationships alive through phone screens. A documentary crew films both sides.

**Overview**

The longest conversation Amira has with her husband Khalil each week lasts eleven minutes. She is in Berlin. He is in Idlib. The connection drops regularly. When it holds, she turns the phone so he can see their daughter eating breakfast. He holds his phone up to the window so she can see the street. Neither of them says anything remarkable. That is the point.

*STILL CONNECTED* is a four-part documentary series built around an observation so common it has become invisible: millions of people now conduct their most important relationships through screens — not by choice, but because war, displacement, and economic collapse put borders between them. The phone call is not a supplement to their life together. It is their life together.

Each episode follows one couple over six months. We film in both locations simultaneously — a split-screen structure that the audience sees from the first frame. The visual grammar of the series is the video call itself: faces lit by phone screens, rooms seen through rear cameras, conversations cut short by signal loss. But the series also films the life that surrounds each call — the eleven minutes Amira and Khalil talk, and the hours on either side when they do not.

**Narrative Arc**

The series is structured as four standalone episodes that accumulate into a larger argument.

Episode 1 establishes the format and the emotional contract. Amira and Khalil are the most conventionally sympathetic couple — a young family separated by a conflict they did not choose, maintaining a marriage across a 4,000-kilometer gap. The episode earns the audience's trust by delivering recognizable emotional material (love, distance, a child growing up between two screens) and then complicating it: Khalil is not waiting to leave. He has chosen to stay.

Episode 2 shifts the dynamic. Olena and Dmytro are separated by a war Dmytro is actively fighting. The calls are briefer. The information asymmetry is extreme — she knows less than he does about his safety, and what he tells her is edited for her protection. The episode examines what intimacy means when one partner is withholding.

Episode 3 introduces a relationship between a parent and an adult child. Nasra, a Somali journalist based in Nairobi, calls her mother Fadumo in Mogadishu daily. The calls are mundane, repetitive, full of the small domestic narration ("I am cooking. The neighbor came. The cat is on the roof.") that constitutes most long-distance communication. The episode is the quietest in the series and the most structurally demanding — it asks the audience to find the stakes in repetition.

Episode 4 follows the youngest subjects. Carmen, a Venezuelan nurse in Bogota, left her sixteen-year-old daughter Sofia in Caracas with Carmen's mother. Sofia is old enough to resent the absence and young enough to need the presence. The calls are volatile, tender, accusatory, forgiving — sometimes within the same three minutes. The episode lands the series on its sharpest emotional terrain.

**Key Subjects**

**Amira and Khalil** (Berlin / Idlib). Married eight years. Amira left Syria three years ago with their daughter; Khalil stayed to manage his family's property. Their relationship is defined by the gap between what they show each other on screen and what they experience off it.

**Olena and Dmytro** (Porto / eastern Ukraine). Married twelve years. Dmytro enlisted in 2022. Olena evacuated to Portugal with their two sons. They speak in short, careful sentences. Neither asks the question they most want answered.

**Nasra and Fadumo** (Nairobi / Mogadishu). Mother and daughter. Nasra left Somalia at nineteen; she is now thirty-eight. Fadumo has never left. Their daily calls are a ritual neither would describe as extraordinary, which is what makes them extraordinary.

**Carmen and Sofia** (Bogota / Caracas). Mother and daughter. Carmen left Venezuela eighteen months ago to work as a nurse. Sofia stayed. The calls are the most emotionally raw material in the series — an adolescent renegotiating her relationship with an absent parent in real time.

**Visual Approach**

Split screen is the native grammar. Both sides of the call appear simultaneously from the first frame. The audience is never on only one side. When a call ends, the split screen persists — showing each person alone in their respective space, separated by the vertical line that divided the frame a moment ago.

We also film in single-camera observational mode when the subjects are not on calls. These sequences provide the context that the calls cannot: Amira's daily life in a Berlin apartment; Khalil's routine in a city under intermittent bombardment; Olena learning Portuguese; Dmytro on rotation. The rhythm of the series alternates between the compressed, face-lit intimacy of the calls and the wider, colder reality that surrounds them.

No presenter. No expert talking heads. No explanatory graphics. The couples speak. The images speak. The structure does the editorial work.

**Why Now / Production Context**

The pandemic normalized screen-mediated relationships for everyone. That normalization has faded for most people but remains permanent for the millions whose separation is political, not epidemiological. The series arrives at a moment when audiences have personal experience of screen-only intimacy — and can be asked to consider what it means to live inside that condition indefinitely, not for eighteen months.

*STILL CONNECTED* is in active development. Screen-recorded material from three of the four couples exists. The director has spent time with Amira, Olena, and Carmen in their respective cities. Access to Khalil is mediated through Amira's phone — which is itself part of the film's method. Nasra and Fadumo have agreed in principle. Production is proposed over twelve months with two-person crews deployed to each location.

**Series Bible**

**Series Title:** STILL CONNECTED
**Format:** 4 x 52 minutes
**Genre:** Observational documentary series
**Primary platform:** Netflix / Apple TV+

**Series Premise**

Four couples. Four conflict zones. Four phone screens. The series films both sides of long-distance relationships maintained across war, displacement, and economic collapse — not as a survey of geopolitics, but as a record of what intimacy looks like when it has been compressed to a rectangle and a signal.

**Tone and Visual Identity**

Intimate. Emotionally direct. The visual signature is the split screen — both sides of every call visible simultaneously. Cinematography alternates between the phone-screen register (faces in screen-light, rear-camera room scans, pixelated freeze-frames when signal drops) and observational sequences shot in available light in each location. The series does not explain the conflicts that separated these couples; it trusts the audience to bring that context. The editorial position is domestic, not geopolitical.

**Episode Breakdown**

**Episode 1: AMIRA & KHALIL (Berlin / Idlib) — "The Eleven-Minute Window"**
Amira and Khalil get eleven minutes a week, sometimes less. The episode opens on a call that drops after ninety seconds and follows Amira through the hours she spends not calling — working, parenting, waiting. We establish the split-screen grammar: when Amira calls back, both sides appear. Khalil shows her the street from his window. She shows him their daughter's drawing. In the episode's emotional center, Amira asks when he will apply for asylum. He says he will not leave. The episode lands on the disconnect between what the phone can carry and what it cannot: Amira knows her husband's face in screen-light better than she knows it in daylight.

**Episode 2: OLENA & DMYTRO (Porto / eastern Ukraine) — "What He Doesn't Say"**
The briefest calls, the widest silences. Dmytro is on rotation near the front. Olena teaches their sons Portuguese in a rented apartment. Their calls are careful — he edits what he tells her, she edits what she asks. The episode tracks the information asymmetry: what she learns from the call, what she learns from the news, what she learns from his face when he forgets the camera is on. In the episode's sharpest sequence, their older son joins a call and asks his father when the war will end. Dmytro answers. Olena watches him answer. The camera watches Olena.

**Episode 3: NASRA & FADUMO (Nairobi / Mogadishu) — "The Daily Call"**
The quietest episode. Nasra calls her mother every morning. The calls are short, repetitive, full of small domestic reporting. The episode asks the audience to find the stakes in routine — to hear, beneath the repeated "I am cooking, the cat is on the roof," the negotiation of a relationship that has been conducted at distance for nineteen years. Fadumo has never visited Nairobi. Nasra has not returned to Mogadishu since she left. The physical distance has become structural, permanent, and the daily call is not a bridge across it but a way of living inside it.

**Episode 4: CARMEN & SOFIA (Bogota / Caracas) — "The Fight"**
The most emotionally volatile episode. Carmen and Sofia argue, reconcile, go silent, and call back — sometimes within an hour. Sofia is sixteen, old enough to articulate the accusation ("you left") and young enough to need the reassurance ("I love you, come back"). Carmen works twelve-hour nursing shifts and calls in the gaps. The episode builds to a conversation that neither of them planned — a late-night call where Sofia, unable to sleep, asks her mother to describe the apartment in Bogota. Carmen holds the phone up and shows her each room. It is the first time Sofia has seen where her mother lives.

**Series Arc**

The four episodes stand alone but accumulate. Episode 1 establishes the format and the emotional territory. Episode 2 introduces danger and withholding. Episode 3 slows the rhythm and redefines intimacy as repetition. Episode 4 delivers the most direct emotional confrontation. Across the series, the audience comes to understand the phone screen not as a communication tool but as a habitat — a place where relationships live because there is nowhere else for them to go.

**Commissioning Rationale**

The series is designed for global streaming platforms seeking authored documentary with international scope and emotional immediacy. Each episode works as a standalone watch (crucial for algorithm-driven platforms where episode order is not guaranteed) while the four together build a cumulative argument. The subject matter is global without requiring geopolitical fluency — the audience enters through the relationships, not the conflicts. The split-screen format is visually distinctive and scalable across promotional materials. Total production window: twelve months.

## Test Case 3: SALVAGE YARD (short documentary, festival circuit)

### Brief

A short documentary (18–22 minutes) about a retired shipbreaker in Chittagong, Bangladesh, who now lives in a house built entirely from materials salvaged from the ships he spent forty years dismantling. The film is a portrait — no narration, no interviews, just the man moving through his house, using objects whose origins he knows. A door handle from an oil tanker. A kitchen counter cut from a ship's deck plate. A window frame from a German container vessel he remembers by name. The film is for the festival circuit — Locarno, Clermont-Ferrand, IDFA. Tone: "Leviathan" meets "Le Quattro Volte." No broadcaster target; this is an art-house short seeking festival premieres and gallery exhibition.

**Pitch Treatment**

**Logline**
A retired shipbreaker lives in a house built entirely from salvaged ship materials. Every object has an origin. He remembers them all.

**Overview**

Rahim does not talk about his work. He talks about the metal.

For forty years he dismantled ships on the breaking beaches at Chittagong — cutting steel with oxy-acetylene torches, hauling plate, sorting fittings. When he retired, he built a house from what he had saved. The door handles came from tankers. The kitchen counter is a section of deck plate, cut to fit, still bearing the anti-slip pattern. The window frames came from a German container vessel — he remembers its name, its tonnage, the year it arrived at the beach.

*SALVAGE YARD* is a short observational portrait. No narration, no interviews, no expository text. The film watches Rahim move through his house. He cooks. He sits. He opens and closes doors. He runs his hand along surfaces. Occasionally he speaks — not to the camera, but to himself or to a visitor — and what he says is always about the object: where it came from, what ship, what voyage it might have made.

The film's argument is carried by its mise en scene, not by speech. The house is a ship turned inside out. Every composition frames Rahim inside a structure whose previous life is visible in its surface — rivets, welds, paint layers, serial numbers stamped into steel. The audience reads the house as they would read a face: marked by time and labor.

**Narrative Structure**

The film follows the duration of a single day. Dawn to dark.

Morning: Rahim wakes, boils water on a stove made from a ship's galley burner, opens the front door — a watertight door from a cargo vessel, still bearing its wheel lock. He steps outside. The camera holds on the door.

Midday: Rahim receives a visitor — a younger man from the breaking beach who has come to ask about a fitting. The conversation is practical, elliptical, and entirely about metal. Rahim identifies a bolt by the ship it came from. The visitor leaves with the bolt and a piece of information he could not have found elsewhere.

Afternoon: Rahim sits in the room with the German window. He looks through it. The film holds on the view — a dusty lane, a corrugated roof, motorbikes — framed by the window that once showed open ocean.

Evening: Rahim closes the house. Every latch and hinge has a specific action, a specific sound. The door wheel turns. The house seals. The final image is the exterior: a structure that looks, in the blue-hour light, like something between a house and a hull.

**Key Subject**

**Rahim, retired shipbreaker.** Approximately seventy. Forty years on the Chittagong breaking beaches. His body carries the work — scarred hands, a stiffness in the shoulders, the economy of movement that comes from decades of physical labor in dangerous conditions. He is not sentimental about ships or about his career. He is precise. He knows materials the way a surgeon knows anatomy: by function, by origin, by how they behave under stress.

**Visual Approach**

Static camera. Long takes. Available light only — the interior of the house is lit by whatever comes through the salvaged windows and the open door. The film's visual register is closer to gallery installation than to broadcast documentary: compositions are held, movement within the frame is slow, cuts are infrequent. Sound design is foregrounded — the specific resonance of metal surfaces, the sound of a watertight door closing, the difference between a steel floor and a concrete one.

No music. No titles until the end. The film trusts its images.

**Why Now / Production Context**

The Chittagong shipbreaking industry is one of the most documented labor stories in photojournalism and documentary. *SALVAGE YARD* approaches it from an oblique angle — not the labor itself, not the hazards, not the economics, but the afterlife of the material and the man who knows it best. Rahim is aging. His house is the only structure of its kind. The film is also a document of a material archive that will not outlast its builder.

The director has visited Rahim twice and received permission to film. A five-day shoot is proposed. Budget is minimal — a two-person crew, available-light shooting, no post-production beyond sound mix and color grade.

**MEDIUM** *(100 words)*

*(95 words)*

**LONG** *(250 words)*

Rahim's front door is a watertight door from a cargo vessel. It still has its wheel lock. He opens it every morning the same way he opened compartments on the breaking beach — with a practiced quarter-turn and a pull that accounts for the weight of marine-grade steel.

His kitchen counter is a section of deck plate, cut to fit, still bearing the anti-slip diamond pattern. His window frames came from a German container vessel; he knows its name, its tonnage, the year it arrived at Chittagong. The stove is a ship's galley burner. The bolts that hold the roof beams are naval specification, pulled from a hull he spent three months dismantling in 2004.

For forty years, Rahim worked the breaking beaches — the stretch of tidal flats where the world's retired ships are cut apart by hand and torch. When he stopped, he built the only house he could afford from the only material he understood.

*SALVAGE YARD* follows a single day inside this structure. Dawn to dark. Rahim cooks, sits, opens and closes doors, receives a visitor from the beach who needs a bolt identified, and watches the lane outside through a window that once showed open ocean. The camera is static. The light is available. The sound is metal on metal, latches turning, water boiling on salvaged steel.

No narration. No music. The film proposes that a house can be a portrait, and that a man's working life can be read in the surfaces that surround him.
