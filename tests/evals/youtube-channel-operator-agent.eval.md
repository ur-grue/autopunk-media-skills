**Channel niche:** Consumer tech reviews (gadgets, electronics)

**Tone:** Fast-paced, opinionated, direct — strong verdicts, no hedging
**Topic:** Reviewing a portable projector for apartment use

**[HOOK — 0:00]**

This is the best portable projector for apartment use right now, and it's not the one every other tech channel is pushing. I've been using it for six months. Here's my honest take.

[On camera, projector on desk, direct address. Fast pace, no preamble.]

I moved into a smaller apartment last year and I didn't want a TV on the wall. So I went down the portable projector rabbit hole. Tested eleven of them. Nine went back. Two survived long enough to form an opinion on, and one of those is the clear winner for how I actually use a projector in an apartment. Not in a home theater. Not in a blacked-out room. In a normal apartment with windows and lamps and a roommate who leaves the kitchen light on.

**[BUILD QUALITY AND PORTS — 1:30]**

The hardware is good. Aluminum body, about the size of a thick paperback, 900 grams. It doesn't feel like a toy, which is already better than most things in this price range. USB-C does power delivery — one cable from my laptop handles video and power. That's the kind of detail that separates a product someone actually designed from a spec-sheet exercise.

Focus is manual. A dial on the side. I know auto-focus is the selling point on most competitors, but auto-focus hunts. It adjusts, re-adjusts, hunts again, settles, then re-adjusts when someone walks past. Manual focus: set it once, done. This is a feature, not a compromise.

[Delivery note: Hold the projector, point out ports while talking. Rotate it. The physical handling sells the build quality better than describing it. Keep the pace — this section should feel like showing a friend something you bought, not reading a spec sheet.]

**[PICTURE QUALITY IN REAL LIGHT — 3:15]**

Here's where most portable projectors fail the apartment test. They look great in a dark room at 9 PM with every light off and the blinds closed. Congratulations — so does my phone pointed at a white wall.

The question is: can I watch something at 4 PM on a Saturday with daylight coming through the window? Because that's when I actually want to use a projector.

At 300 lumens — which is what most competitors in this range claim — the answer is no. The image washes out. You're squinting. It's worse than a laptop screen. This unit puts out closer to 450 ANSI lumens measured, not claimed, and the difference is visible. Not perfect in direct sunlight — nothing at this price is — but genuinely watchable with ambient light. I watched an entire season of a show on a Saturday afternoon without closing the blinds. That's the test.

[Delivery note: Show side-by-side footage — projector in a dark room vs. projector with daylight. Don't oversell the daylight performance, but be clear about the gap between this and the competition. If possible, film the same scene on a competitor at the same time of day.]

## Test Case 3: RAMEN UPGRADES (cooking channel for college students)

### Brief

A cooking YouTube channel targeting college students — approximately 8,000 subscribers. Beginner-friendly, casual and fun tone. The creator has NO specific video topic — they want the agent to generate ideas first, so the full seven-step pipeline runs including step 1 (niche-video-idea-generator). **Channel niche:** Easy cooking for college students on a budget
**Target audience:** College students (18-22) who can barely cook, living in dorms or small apartments with limited kitchen equipment
**Tone:** Casual, fun, self-deprecating — like a friend who figured out how to feed themselves and wants to share

### Simulated deliverable (condensed)

---

**PRODUCTION PACKAGE: 5-MINUTE RAMEN UPGRADES**

---

**Production Summary**

This package covers a 7-minute cooking video about upgrading instant ramen with simple, cheap additions. All seven steps were run — including idea generation, since no topic was provided. The package contains: a summary of the 10 generated video concepts with the selected pick, 3 opening hooks, a complete ~900-word script for 7 minutes, 8 title options, 2 thumbnail concept directions, a paste-ready YouTube description, and 7 chapter timestamps. The script is structured around four upgrades of increasing ambition, giving the video a built-in progression that keeps viewers watching to see the next level.

---

**1. Video Concept (selected from idea generation)**

The agent generated 10 ranked video concepts. The top three:

**#1 — 5-Minute Ramen Upgrades That Actually Taste Good** *(Selected)*
Take instant ramen — the thing you're already eating three nights a week — and show four upgrades using grocery-store ingredients. The angle: don't stop eating ramen, just stop eating boring ramen. Low barrier to entry. Every viewer already has the base ingredient in their cabinet.
*Differentiation: Most ramen-upgrade videos target home cooks who already know their way around a kitchen. This one targets people who consider boiling water a cooking achievement.*

**#2 — One Skillet, One Grocery Trip, Five Dinners**
Buy one set of ingredients and make five different meals across the week using only one pan. Appeals to the viewer who is broke, owns one pan, and is tired of eating the same thing every night.
*Differentiation: Meal-prep videos assume equipment and planning. This assumes one pan and no plan.*

**#3 — The $3 Lunch That Got Me Through Finals Week**
One recipe with a personal story frame. Budget-anchored, emotionally specific, works as both a recipe and a "surviving college" narrative.
*Differentiation: Recipe plus personal story is rare in the college-cooking niche, which tends toward listicles.*

*Selection rationale: Concept #1 was selected for strongest search potential ("ramen upgrades" has consistent search volume in the cooking niche), lowest barrier to entry (the viewer already owns the base ingredient), and highest shareability among college audiences. Concept #2 is the strongest backup for a follow-up video.*

**2. Opening Hooks**

**Hook A — Provocative Question:**
'You've been eating instant ramen wrong your entire college career. Five minutes and about two dollars can fix that.'

**Hook B — Surprising Fact:**
'I spent two dollars and forty cents and turned a twenty-cent packet of ramen into something I'd actually order at a restaurant. Here are four ways to do that.'

**Hook C — Bold Claim:** *(Recommended)*
'Instant ramen is the single best base ingredient in your kitchen and nobody treats it that way. Four upgrades, five minutes each, all under three bucks.'

*Recommendation: Hook C. It reframes the viewer's relationship with a product they already buy — instant ramen goes from a shameful convenience to a smart starting point. **3. Full Script (excerpt — opening + two representative sections)**

*Approximate word count for full script: 900 words | Estimated runtime: ~7 minutes at casual pace*

**[HOOK — 0:00]**

Instant ramen is the single best base ingredient in your kitchen and nobody treats it that way. Four upgrades, five minutes each, all under three bucks.

[On camera, dorm kitchen or small apartment kitchen. Casual, standing at the counter, packet of ramen in hand.]

OK so here's the thing. I'm not going to stand here and tell you to stop eating instant ramen. That would be obnoxious and also hypocritical because I ate it last night. Ramen is cheap, it's fast, and when you get home at 11 PM from the library and you haven't eaten since that sad dining hall sandwich at noon, it's what you're going to make. I get it. So instead of pretending you're going to start meal-prepping on Sundays — you're not — let's just make the ramen better.

**[UPGRADE 1: THE EGG DROP — 0:45]**

Easiest one first. Make your ramen the normal way — boil water, add noodles, add the flavor packet. But in the last thirty seconds, crack an egg straight into the pot and stir it around. The egg breaks apart into these little ribbons and makes the broth actually thick and filling instead of basically flavored hot water.

That's it. One egg. You already have eggs. If you don't have eggs, this is your sign to buy eggs.

Now if you want to be slightly fancy about it — and I mean slightly — soft-boil the egg instead. Six and a half minutes in boiling water, then straight into cold water, peel it, cut it in half, drop it on top. You now have something that looks like it costs fourteen dollars at a ramen bar, and it cost you maybe fifty cents more than the plain version.

[Delivery note: Do this one fast. The simplicity is the point. Don't over-explain the egg-ribbon technique — show it, don't lecture about it. The soft-boil upgrade is the "level two" moment. Play the contrast for gentle comedy: the amount of effort is still embarrassingly low, but the result looks impressive.]

**[UPGRADE 3: THE PEANUT BUTTER MOVE — 3:15]**

OK this is the one that sounds weird and I need you to trust me for thirty seconds.
