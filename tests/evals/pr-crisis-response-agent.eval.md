## Test Case 2: NOVAPAY DATA BREACH

### Brief

NovaPay is a mid-sized fintech startup (~200 employees) based in London. The company provides payment processing and digital wallet services to small businesses and consumers across the UK.

On Wednesday evening, NovaPay's security team detected unauthorized access to a customer database. The affected database contains customer names, email addresses, last four digits of payment cards, and transaction history. Approximately 12,000 customer records were accessed. There is no evidence of financial fraud or full card number exposure at this time.

NovaPay's incident response team has contained the breach and engaged an external cybersecurity firm to conduct a forensic investigation. The ICO (Information Commissioner's Office) has been notified within the 72-hour window required under UK GDPR.

**Designated spokesperson:** Amir Patel, CEO and Co-Founder
**Legal constraints:** Legal counsel says NovaPay must not confirm or speculate about the attack vector (how the breach occurred) until the forensic investigation is complete. They can confirm: the breach occurred, the categories of data accessed, that the ICO has been notified, and that the breach has been contained.
**Media status:** No media coverage yet. No journalist enquiries received.
**Proactive media outreach:** No -- the strategy is to minimise media attention. Respond to enquiries if they come but do not pitch the story.
**Press release:** Yes -- a formal statement is warranted for the regulatory record and for transparency with affected customers.
**Tone:** Direct, technically credible, and calm. NovaPay is a technology company; the communications should reflect competence and transparency without corporate defensiveness.

**3. FAQ Document**

### External FAQ -- Customers and Media

**Q: What data was accessed?**
Customer names, email addresses, the last four digits of payment cards, and transaction history. No full card numbers, CVV codes, passwords, or bank account details were accessed.

**Q: How many customers are affected?**
Approximately 12,000 customer records were in the accessed database.

**Q: Has my money been stolen?**
We have found no evidence of financial fraud resulting from this breach. The accessed database did not contain the information needed to make payments -- no full card numbers, no CVV codes, no passwords.

**Q: How did this happen?**
We have engaged an external cybersecurity firm to determine how the unauthorized access occurred. Until that investigation is complete, we cannot comment on the method of access.

**Q: Why should I trust NovaPay with my data after this?**
We detected the breach through our own security monitoring and contained it within hours. We notified the ICO, engaged independent investigators, and are being transparent about what happened. Trust is rebuilt through actions, and we intend to demonstrate that through the investigation findings and the changes we make as a result.

**Q: Are you offering compensation?**
We are providing 12 months of free credit monitoring to all affected customers. We will continue to assess what further support is appropriate as the investigation progresses.

**Q: Could the last four digits of my card be used for fraud?**
The last four digits alone are not sufficient to make transactions. However, combined with other information an attacker might hold from other sources, they could be used in social engineering attacks -- for instance, a caller claiming to be your bank and quoting the last four digits to gain your trust. Be sceptical of any unsolicited contact that references your financial details.

**Q: Has the ICO taken any enforcement action?**
No. We notified the ICO within the required timeframe and are cooperating fully with their office. The ICO's investigation is ongoing.

### Internal FAQ -- Staff

**Q: What happened?**

The affected database contained customer records only. Staff HR data, payroll information, and internal systems were not involved. If this changes as the investigation proceeds, you will be informed immediately.

**Q: What should I say if a customer asks me about it?**
Direct them to the security incident page at novapay.co.uk/security-update or the incident helpline at [phone number]. **4. Spokesperson Briefing**

*For: Amir Patel, CEO and Co-Founder*
*Prepared for: reactive media enquiries and potential investor conversations on [date]*

### Key Messages (maximum three)

1. **We found it, we contained it, and we are being transparent.** 'Our security team detected this breach through our own monitoring systems. We contained it within hours, notified the ICO, and are now telling our customers directly what happened and what data was involved.'

2. **The most sensitive financial data was not exposed.** 'No full card numbers, no CVV codes, no passwords, no bank account details. The accessed database held names, email addresses, the last four digits of cards, and transaction history.'

3. **We are taking specific steps to protect affected customers.** 'We are providing 12 months of credit monitoring, we have published clear guidance on what to watch for, and our incident helpline is open.'

### Q&A Preparation

**Q: How did the attackers get in?**
Bridge: 'That is exactly what the forensic investigation is determining. I cannot speculate on the method of access until we have the findings, but what I can tell you is that we detected the breach through our own monitoring, contained it within hours, and engaged an independent cybersecurity firm.'

**Q: Twelve thousand records -- how is that not a massive failure for a company that handles payments?**
Direct answer: 'It is a serious incident and we are treating it as one. No payment processing system was compromised and no full card numbers were exposed, but 12,000 customers had personal data accessed without authorization. That is unacceptable to us, which is why we are being direct about it rather than minimising it.'

**Q: Are you just another fintech that moved fast and broke things with people's data?**
Bridge: 'I understand the scepticism. I would rather be judged on what we do from here -- the speed of detection, the transparency of disclosure, the support we are giving affected customers, and the changes we make once the investigation concludes.'

### Landmines -- Do Not Say

- **Do not speculate on the attacker's identity or method.** "It looks like it was probably a vulnerability in..." becomes the headline. Legal has been clear: until the forensic investigation reports, the attack vector is unknown.
- **Do not say "no harm was done."** Transaction history and partial card details combined with data from other breaches can enable social engineering and identity fraud. The risk is real even if full card numbers were not exposed.
- **Do not say "our security is best-in-class" or any equivalent.** A company that just disclosed a breach cannot claim superior security. If asked about your security posture, say: 'The investigation will tell us where we fell short, and we will act on those findings.'
- **Do not minimise the number.** Twelve thousand is a significant number of people. Do not compare it favourably to larger breaches ("at least it wasn't millions"). Every affected customer is one too many.
- **Do not promise it will not happen again** until the investigation identifies the cause and remediation is in place.
- **Do not discuss specific employees, roles, or internal responsibilities.** The forensic investigation will determine what happened.
