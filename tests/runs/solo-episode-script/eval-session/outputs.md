Second: the narrative fallacy. We are storytelling animals. We want our lives to have coherent plots. **[02:00–06:00] CONTEXT: HOW MERCATOR BECAME THE DEFAULT**

To understand how we got here, we need to go back to 1569. That's when a Flemish cartographer named Gerardus Mercator published the map projection that bears his name.

Mercator was solving a genuine problem. Sailors needed a flat map where a straight line between two points corresponded to a constant compass bearing — what navigators call a rhumb line. If you drew a line on a Mercator map from Lisbon to the Caribbean and measured the angle, you could hold that compass heading and you'd arrive. For sixteenth-century navigation, that was revolutionary.

[PAUSE]

The cost of that navigational convenience was distortion. To make compass bearings work as straight lines, the Mercator projection stretches landmasses progressively as they move away from the equator. Near the equator, the map is reasonably accurate. Near the poles, it inflates everything enormously. That's why Greenland looks the size of Africa. That's why Alaska looks larger than Mexico, when Mexico is actually larger. That's why the entire continent of Antarctica — which is about the size of the United States and Mexico combined — looks like it spans the bottom of the world as an infinite white band.

Here's the thing: the Mercator projection was designed for ocean navigation. It was never intended to be the standard wall map, the default classroom map, the backdrop for news broadcasts. It was a tool for a specific purpose, and it was excellent at that purpose.

But somewhere between the sixteenth century and the twentieth, it became the default. It became the image of the world. And because it became the default in European and North American classrooms, offices, and media — the places where global political and economic decisions are made — its distortions became, in a sense, invisible.

---

**[06:00–11:30] SEGMENT ONE: THE DISTORTION IS NOT NEUTRAL**

Now, you might say: it's a map. All flat maps distort a sphere. That's mathematics, not politics.

That's true. Every projection involves trade-offs. You cannot flatten a sphere without distorting something — area, shape, distance, or direction. The question is not whether a map distorts, but what it distorts, and who benefits from that distortion.

[SLOW DOWN HERE]

The Mercator projection distorts area. Specifically, it inflates the area of landmasses at high latitudes — which is to say, it inflates Europe, North America, and Russia — and compresses the area of landmasses near the equator, which is to say, it shrinks Africa, South America, South and Southeast Asia, and the Pacific Islands.

This is not a conspiracy theory. There was no meeting where European cartographers decided to make Europe look bigger. But the effect is real. A European colonial power looking at a Mercator map saw its territories looking substantial, important, dominant. The colonies looked smaller, more manageable, less significant than they actually are.

[PAUSE]

And the distortion compounds. When you grow up seeing Africa as roughly the same size as Greenland on every map you encounter — in school, on the news, in atlases — you internalize a spatial model of the world that is wrong. Not symbolically wrong. Metrically wrong. Your intuitive sense of scale is miscalibrated.

Research supports this. A study published in the *Journal of Geography* asked university students in Europe and North America to estimate the relative sizes of continents. Respondents consistently underestimated the size of Africa by a factor of two to three. They overestimated Europe by a comparable margin. When shown the true proportions, many expressed genuine surprise.

**[11:30–17:00] SEGMENT TWO: ALTERNATIVE PROJECTIONS AND THE POLITICS OF THE SWITCH**

The Mercator projection was not the only option. It was never the only option.

In 1855, a Scottish clergyman named James Gall published an equal-area cylindrical projection. In 1974, the German historian Arno Peters independently published what was essentially the same projection and launched a public campaign to replace Mercator in schools and media. The Gall-Peters projection preserves area accurately — Africa looks the right size relative to Europe. But it distorts shape: countries near the equator appear stretched vertically, and countries at high latitudes appear squashed.

[PAUSE]

The response from the cartographic establishment was hostile. Professional cartographers pointed out — correctly — that the Gall-Peters projection introduces its own severe distortions of shape. They argued — also correctly — that Peters had essentially reinvented Gall's work and marketed it as his own. They were not wrong about the technical criticisms.

But they missed the political point. Peters was not primarily making a cartographic argument. He was making a political one: that the default image of the world in Western institutions systematically diminishes the visual presence of the Global South, and that this matters.

When news broadcasts show geopolitical events, the backdrop is usually Mercator. There are now dozens of alternative projections in common use. The Robinson projection, which the National Geographic Society used from 1988 to 1998, compromises between area and shape — nothing is perfectly accurate, but nothing is wildly distorted either. The Winkel tripel, which National Geographic adopted in 1998, takes a similar approach. The AuthaGraph projection, developed by the Japanese architect Hajime Narukawa in 1999, folds the globe into a tetrahedron before unfolding it — the result is visually unfamiliar but remarkably accurate in area.

None of these has replaced Mercator as the default. The inertia is enormous. Mercator is what people expect a map to look like. It's what tile-based web mapping systems like Google Maps use for their base layer. It's what classrooms have on the wall. Replacing it requires not just a better projection but a willingness to tell people that the image of the world they've carried since childhood is substantively wrong.

But the visual shorthand of the default world map compresses those differences. It makes the vast look manageable and the distant look close.

I'm not suggesting that anyone consciously decides to underfund African programs because of a map. The map becomes the territory — not literally, but cognitively.

The practical takeaway is not "throw out your atlas." It's to develop what I'd call cartographic literacy — an awareness that every map has a projection, every projection has a distortion, and the choice of projection is never neutral.
