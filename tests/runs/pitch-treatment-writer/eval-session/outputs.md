## Output 1 (input-01): Standard documentary pitch

**Input:** A feature documentary following a retired teacher who has spent 15 years photographing every abandoned school building in her state, documenting the architectural decay and the communities that were hollowed out when the schools closed. Format: Single feature documentary, ~75 minutes. Target broadcaster: Independent documentary strand. Tone reference: Quiet observational, like Nomadland or Minari. Primary subject: Margaret Hollis, 72, retired elementary school teacher. Central question: What happens to a community's identity when its school disappears?

---

**Logline**
A retired teacher has spent fifteen years photographing every abandoned school in her state — not to preserve the buildings, but to document what happened to the towns that lost them.

---

**Overview**

A school closing is an administrative event. What follows is not. When a school shuts its doors, the building stays — for a while. Then the windows break. Then the roof goes. Then the town that built it starts to look like the building it no longer maintains.

Margaret Hollis knows this because she has seen it 136 times. Over the past fifteen years, the 72-year-old retired elementary school teacher has driven to every abandoned school building in her state, photographing the decay — and, more importantly, talking to the people who live in its shadow. Her archive is not an architectural record. It is a record of community collapse, told through the structures that communities once built to hold themselves together.

*EMPTY ROOMS* follows Margaret across one year of her continuing project. We travel with her to the towns, meet the people who stayed, and see what a school looks like ten, twenty, thirty years after the last child leaves.

---

**Narrative Arc**

We open in Margaret's home, where the archive lives: filing cabinets of photographs, organized by county, each school labeled with its closure date and the population of the town at the time. She pulls a file and describes the school it contains with the precision of someone who has stood inside the building. We understand immediately that this is not a hobby. It is a sustained act of documentation.

The first act follows Margaret to three sites. At each, the pattern is the same: she photographs the building, walks the town, and talks to residents. What emerges is a taxonomy of loss. The grocery store that followed the school. The post office that followed the grocery store. The families that stayed and the ones that did not. Margaret listens more than she speaks.

In the second act, the film turns inward. Margaret visits the school where she taught for twenty-eight years — now closed for seven. She has photographed it before, but this visit is different: the roof has partially collapsed. She photographs the damage with the same careful method, but for the first time in the film, she stops mid-sentence. The building is becoming unrecognizable.

The final act takes us to a town where the opposite is happening. A community group has purchased its abandoned school and is converting it into a shared civic space. Margaret photographs this too. The film does not present this as a solution — one conversion does not reverse a pattern — but it allows the audience to see what Margaret sees: that the buildings are proxies for a question about whether communities can survive the loss of their gathering places.

---

**Key Subjects**

**Margaret Hollis, 72, Retired Elementary School Teacher.** Twenty-eight years in the classroom, fifteen years on the road with a camera. Precise, unhurried, and deeply uncomfortable with the word "activist." She describes her project as "accounting" — she is counting what was lost so that someone, eventually, can add it up. She drives a pickup truck with 200,000 miles on it and files every photograph by hand.

**Community residents** encountered at each site. Not recurring characters, but voices that accumulate. A former school custodian who still mows the lawn of a building that closed in 2009. A town clerk who can recite the enrollment numbers for every year the school was open. These are not interviews — they are conversations Margaret initiates, and the film observes.

---

**Visual Approach**

The film is observational, quiet, and visually led. Margaret's photographs — still images of the buildings — are integrated as full-screen inserts, held long enough to study. The filmmaking mirrors her method: patient, precise, and respectful of the places it documents. No drone footage. No time-lapses. The camera stays at human height, in the spaces people actually inhabit. Archive footage of the schools in operation — yearbook photos, local news clips — appears only when Margaret herself references it.

---

**Why Now / Production Context**

Across the country, rural school consolidation continues to accelerate. Since 2010, more than 4,000 rural school buildings have been closed or consolidated. The communities affected rarely make national news. Margaret's archive — one of the most comprehensive individual documentation projects of its kind — is now large enough to constitute a historical record. The film captures both the archive and the person behind it while the work is still active. *EMPTY ROOMS* is in active development. Initial filming access with Margaret has been established.

## Output 2 (input-02): Niche technical subject

**Input:** A documentary about the people who maintain undersea internet cables — the physical infrastructure that carries 99% of intercontinental data. The film follows a cable ship crew during a repair mission in the North Atlantic. Format: Single documentary, ~90 minutes. Target broadcaster: Science/technology documentary strand. Central question: What happens when the invisible infrastructure of the modern world breaks? Primary subjects: The ship's captain and the lead cable engineer.

---

**Logline**
Ninety-nine percent of the world's intercontinental data travels through cables on the ocean floor. When one breaks, a single ship and its crew are the only way to fix it.

---

**Overview**

Every email, every video call, every financial transaction that crosses an ocean travels through a cable thinner than a garden hose, lying on the seabed. There are over 500 of these cables worldwide. When one fails, the repair is not digital. It is mechanical, physical, and performed by a small number of specialized ships that can locate the fault, retrieve the cable from depths of up to 8,000 meters, and splice it back together at sea.

*THE DEEP FIX* follows the crew of one cable repair ship during a single mission in the North Atlantic. A subsea cable carrying data between two continents has faulted. The ship has been dispatched. The repair will take between ten days and three weeks, depending on weather, depth, and the nature of the break. The film stays on the ship for the duration.

---

**Narrative Arc**

We open in the ship's operations room. A fault has been reported. The crew assembles, and the captain — a veteran of twenty years in cable maintenance — briefs the team on the mission. A map shows the cable's route across the Atlantic floor. The fault is at 3,200 meters. We understand immediately that this repair takes place in a world most people will never see.

The first act builds the ship as a world: the cable hold, where kilometers of spare cable wait on massive drums; the engine room; the crew quarters; the bridge. We meet the lead cable engineer, who explains the physics of the work — how a cable is grappled from the seabed using a hook dragged across the ocean floor, how the broken ends are brought to the surface, how the splice is made. The process is manual, physical, and depends on conditions the crew cannot control.

In the second act, the repair begins. The grappling operation does not go smoothly — the first attempt misses the cable. Weather closes in. The ship holds position. The crew waits. This is the reality of the work: long stretches of mechanical repetition interrupted by moments where the entire operation depends on a single maneuver. The lead engineer and the captain disagree on timing. The tension is professional, not personal — both are right, and both know the stakes.

The third act is the splice itself. The cable ends are on deck. The engineer and a small team work inside a sealed environment to join the optical fibers — a process that requires precision measurable in microns, performed on a ship that is moving. When the splice is complete, the cable is tested and lowered back to the seabed. The data flows again. Nobody onshore notices.

---

**Key Subjects**

**The Captain.** Twenty years in cable ship operations. Calm, experienced, and accustomed to making decisions in conditions where the variables are weather, depth, and time. He speaks about the ocean with the matter-of-fact familiarity of someone who has spent most of his adult life on it. He keeps a personal log of every repair mission — not for the company, but for himself.

**The Lead Cable Engineer.** The technical authority on board. She has performed over forty deep-water splices. Methodical, exacting, and visibly focused during the splice sequence. She explains the work without simplifying it — her descriptions make the precision of the operation tangible.

---

**Visual Approach**

The film is contained within the ship and the sea. The visual language alternates between the mechanical scale of the operation — cables, winches, deck machinery — and the microscopic precision of the splice. Underwater remotely operated vehicle footage shows the cable on the seabed, providing the only images of the environment where the infrastructure actually lives. The ship itself provides the visual structure: bridge, deck, cable hold, splice room. No graphics or animations. The real machinery and the real ocean are more compelling than any reconstruction.

---

**Why Now / Production Context**

Geopolitical attention to undersea cable security has increased sharply. Governments and defense agencies now publicly acknowledge the vulnerability of subsea cable networks to sabotage and natural disaster. Yet the people who actually maintain this infrastructure remain invisible to the public. *THE DEEP FIX* makes the human labor behind digital connectivity visible for the first time. The film is in development. A cable ship operator has agreed in principle to filming access during a scheduled North Atlantic repair season.

---

---

**Note:** The input provides a subject (urban beekeeping), a format (short documentary, 30 minutes), and a target (festival), but no central character, specific conflict, or driving question. The treatment below invents a plausible focus to demonstrate what a complete treatment looks like — but you should replace the specifics with your actual subject, location, and story before submitting anywhere.

---

**Logline**
In a city where green space is measured in square meters, a small network of rooftop beekeepers is discovering what their hives reveal about the neighborhoods below.

---

**Overview**

Urban beekeeping is often presented as a feel-good story: bees on rooftops, honey in jars, nature returning to the city. The reality is more complicated — and more interesting.

Bees are not neutral occupants of urban space. Their foraging patterns, their health, and the composition of their honey reflect the environment they live in: pollution levels, pesticide use in parks and gardens, the availability of flowering plants, the temperature of the rooftop. A hive is, in effect, a sensor — and what it measures is the city itself.

*ROOFTOP SEASON* follows three urban beekeepers over one spring-to-autumn season. Each keeps hives in a different part of the city, in a different context: one on a social housing block, one on a restaurant rooftop, one on a community garden shed. The honey is different. The bees behave differently. The film asks what these differences tell us about the neighborhoods the bees inhabit.

---

**Narrative Arc**

We open in early spring. The first beekeeper opens a hive after winter — inspecting the colony, assessing losses. The ritual is practical, not sentimental: some colonies survive the winter and some do not. The season begins.

Through spring and summer, the film tracks the three hives. Each visit reveals something about the environment: one colony thrives near a park; another struggles in an area with heavy traffic and limited forage. The beekeepers compare notes, test honey, and notice patterns. The film builds its argument through accumulation, not narration — the hives themselves are the evidence.

In autumn, the season ends. The honey is harvested. A local laboratory analyzes samples from each hive, and the results make visible what the beekeepers suspected: the honey is a chemical portrait of each neighborhood. The film closes on the hives being sealed for winter — and the city continuing around them.

---

**Key Subjects**

**Three urban beekeepers** in different city locations. Each brings a different relationship to the practice: one is a retired environmental scientist who keeps data on every hive inspection; one is a restaurant owner who uses the honey in his kitchen; one is a community organizer who started beekeeping as a neighborhood project. Their approaches are different, but the bees connect them.

---

**Visual Approach**

Close, observational, and intimate with the hives. Macro photography of the bees and comb. Wide shots that establish each rooftop in its urban context — the skyline, the street below, the surrounding buildings. No presenter. No voice-over unless the filmmaker decides otherwise. The beekeepers' own voices carry the story. The visual rhythm mirrors the season: slow in spring, dense in summer, still in autumn.

---

**Why Now / Production Context**

Urban beekeeping has expanded rapidly in the last decade, but most coverage treats it as a novelty. The scientific literature on bees as bioindicators — living sensors of environmental quality — has grown significantly, offering a rigorous frame for a story that is often told as lifestyle content. *ROOFTOP SEASON* is in early development. Festival submission is the primary target.

---

## Output 4 (input-04): European/international context

**Input:** A documentary following three families in Thessaloniki, Greece, who lost their homes during the 2010s economic crisis and have been living in squatted buildings since. Now the buildings are being sold to developers as part of the post-crisis recovery. Format: Feature documentary, ~85 minutes. Target broadcaster: European public broadcaster (ARTE or similar). Central question: When a city recovers economically, who gets left behind? Tone: Intimate, observational, with a structural argument about gentrification and displacement. Why now: Several squatted buildings in Thessaloniki are scheduled for demolition in the next 12 months.

---

**Logline**
Three families in Thessaloniki have lived in squatted buildings since the economic crisis. Now the city is recovering — and the buildings are being sold to the developers who will tear them down.

---

**Overview**

Economic recovery does not arrive evenly. In Thessaloniki, the cranes are back. Construction projects are underway. Property prices are rising. And in the buildings that the crisis emptied — the ones that sat vacant long enough for displaced families to move in, build lives, and raise children — eviction notices are appearing on the doors.

*STILL HERE* follows three families who have occupied squatted buildings in Thessaloniki since the worst years of the Greek economic crisis. They did not choose this housing as a political act. They chose it because there was nothing else. Over the years, the buildings became homes: repaired, furnished, filled with the routines of daily life. Now the post-crisis property market wants them back.

The film covers one year — the twelve months during which several of these buildings are scheduled for demolition. It asks the question that recovery narratives do not: when a city recovers, who pays?

---

**Narrative Arc**

We open on an ordinary morning in one of the squatted buildings. A family is getting children ready for school. The apartment is maintained, lived-in, and clearly a home. A notice on the building's entrance — partially torn — announces a forthcoming sale. The family does not discuss it at breakfast. We understand that the threat is constant and that daily life continues around it.

The first act introduces the three families and the three buildings. Each family arrived during a different phase of the crisis: one in 2011, at the height of the emergency; one in 2014, during austerity; one in 2016, when the political situation had stabilized but affordable housing had not returned. The buildings are in different parts of the city, in different conditions, and face different timelines. What connects them is the same structural reality: they exist because the market abandoned the buildings, and now the market wants them back.

The second act turns on the arrival of the developers. Letters are delivered. Legal proceedings begin. The families respond differently: one engages a lawyer through a housing advocacy group; one begins looking for alternative accommodation, knowing they cannot afford what is available; one refuses to leave and prepares for a confrontation. The film does not judge these responses. It observes them.

In the final act, the first demolition date arrives. One family has already moved — to a smaller, more precarious rental. One family remains, waiting for a court decision. One building stands empty, its residents dispersed. The film does not provide a resolution because the situation has not resolved. What it offers instead is a precise portrait of what recovery looks like from the inside of a building that recovery is about to erase.

---

**Key Subjects**

**The Karadimou Family.** Arrived in 2011. Two parents, three children, the youngest born in the building. Maria Karadimou works part-time cleaning offices. She speaks about the squat with the practical clarity of someone who has run a household in a building with intermittent electricity for over a decade. The children have never lived anywhere else.

**The Petridis Family.** Arrived in 2016. Yiannis Petridis, a former construction worker, lost his job and then his apartment in the same year. He is the most politically engaged of the three subjects — he attends housing meetings, speaks at demonstrations, and frames the eviction as a systemic issue, not a personal one.

**The Osman Family.** Arrived in 2014. A family of refugee background who were already housing-insecure before the crisis. Fatima Osman is quiet, guarded with the camera, and focused on a single objective: finding stable housing for her two daughters before the building is sold. The film earns her trust slowly.

---

**Visual Approach**

Intimate and interior. The camera stays inside the buildings and inside the families' daily routines. The city's recovery — the construction sites, the renovated storefronts, the rising skyline — is visible only through windows, from balconies, and in passing. This visual strategy mirrors the families' experience: they see the recovery happening around them, but they are not inside it. No talking-head interviews. No archive footage of the crisis. The film is set in the present — what matters is what is happening now.

---

**Why Now / Production Context**

Several squatted buildings in Thessaloniki are scheduled for demolition or sale within the next twelve months. The window to film these families in these spaces is closing. Greece's post-crisis property boom has attracted international investment, but the displacement of crisis-era squatters has received almost no coverage outside local media. ARTE and similar European public broadcasters have commissioned documentaries on housing and displacement — this film fits that editorial context precisely. *STILL HERE* is in active development. Filming relationships with two of the three families have been established.
