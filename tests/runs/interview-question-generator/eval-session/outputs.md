## Output 1 (input-01): Standard documentary interview — Pharmaceutical whistleblower

**OPENING — Establishing Voice and World** *(Goal: Let the subject settle into the interview and find their register before approaching the core material.)*

1. You spent eleven years at this company before anything happened. What was it like working there in the years before you filed the complaint — what did the job mean to you?
2. When you describe what you did at the company to someone outside the industry — your neighbour, a friend at dinner — how do you explain it?
3. Walk me through a normal day during the period when you were still inside the company and hadn't yet reported anything. What did your working life look like?
4. What drew you to pharmaceutical work in the first place — was there a version of the work that you believed in?

*(Production note: Question 4 often reveals the gap between what the subject believed they were doing and what they discovered was happening. Let it run — the disillusionment arc often starts here.)*

---

**SUBSTANTIVE — The Discovery and the Decision** *(Goal: Build the chronology so the subject is narrating rather than defending. Move from what they saw to what they did, letting the story unfold.)*

5. Take me to the period when you first noticed something was wrong with the trial data. What specifically did you see, and where were you when you saw it?
6. When you first encountered the problem, did you think it was an error or did you think it was deliberate? How long did it take for that assessment to change?
7. Who did you talk to first inside the company — and what did they say?
8. Describe the internal conversations you had before going outside. Without naming anyone you cannot name, what was the tone of those conversations?
9. At what point did you understand that this was not going to be resolved internally — that you were going to have to take it outside?
10. What information did you have at the moment you decided to report? What were you weighing — professionally, personally, financially?
11. Walk me through the mechanics of actually filing the complaint. Where did you go? Who did you contact? What does that process actually look like from the inside?
12. What did you expect would happen after you filed?
13. How long was it between filing the complaint and losing your job?
14. When the termination came — how did you first learn about it? Where were you?
15. What happened to your daily life in the weeks after you were terminated? Not the legal process — your actual life.

*(Production note: Question 15 is where the personal cost becomes tangible. Subjects often pause here. Do not fill the silence.)*

16. Is there anything about how the regulatory investigation has been conducted that you think the public should understand about how these processes actually work?

---

**HIGH-STAKES — Cost and Reckoning** *(Goal: Create space for the subject to express doubt, regret, or conviction about the choice they made. These questions arrive only after trust is established.)*

17. Knowing everything that has happened since — the termination, the investigation, the legal exposure — would you make the same decision again?
18. What has this cost you that you did not expect it to cost?
19. There are people who see data they believe is wrong and choose not to report it. What do you understand about that choice now that you did not understand before?
20. When the investigation concludes — however it concludes — what do you need to have happened for this to feel like it was worth it?
21. Is there anything about this experience that you have never been asked and that you want on the record?

*(Production note: Question 19 is the most important question in the set. It invites empathy for the people who stayed silent, which is a far more revealing answer than self-justification. Give it space. Question 21 should be saved for the very end.)*

---

**Next Step:** If time is short, prioritize questions 5, 17, and 19 — they cover the discovery, the personal reckoning, and the most revealing reflection. Before filming, confirm the subject's legal counsel has reviewed any questions touching on the ongoing investigation. Ensure release forms are signed and that the subject understands what can and cannot be discussed given pending proceedings. Consider running scene-breakdown-writer to plan the visual coverage around this interview — particularly the transition from the professional setting to the personal cost material.

## Output 2 (input-02): Niche technical subject — Volcanologist

**OPENING — Establishing Voice and World** *(Goal: Let the subject settle into the interview and establish their relationship with the work before we approach the false alarm.)*

1. You have been watching this volcano for eighteen years. What does a day of monitoring actually look like — what are you reading, measuring, listening for?
2. How do you explain to someone who has never thought about it what it means to monitor a volcano that sits above a city of 300,000 people?
3. When did you first come to this volcano, and what was it about this particular mountain that kept you here for nearly two decades?
4. What does the volcano look like on a quiet day versus a day when something is changing? Walk me through what shifts in the data.

*(Production note: Question 4 is a world-building question. It gives the audience visual and sensory language for understanding what changes look like on the instruments — material the editor will need for the observational sequences.)*

---

**SUBSTANTIVE — The Science and the Weight** *(Goal: Move from the professional to the personal, building toward the false alarm through the context of uncertainty rather than arriving at it as an accusation.)*

5. When people hear "the eruption is statistically overdue," what does that actually mean in scientific terms? How precise is that prediction?
6. Describe the gap between what the data can tell you and what the city needs to hear. How do you bridge that?
7. What is the worst-case scenario for this volcano, and how often do you think about it?
8. When you issue a warning — walk me through what happens. Who do you call first? What is the chain of communication between your instruments and the people who live below?
9. How much time would people have if the worst-case scenario began?
10. What is the hardest part of your job that has nothing to do with the science itself?
11. Take me to the period six years ago — before the evacuation was called. What were your instruments telling you in the days leading up to your recommendation?
12. At what point did you decide that the data warranted an evacuation warning? What were you weighing?
13. Walk me through what happened after the evacuation was called and the eruption did not come. What was that day like for you?
14. How did the public response to the false alarm change your relationship with the community?

*(Production note: Question 13 is the emotional centre of the interview. The subject will likely have a strong physical memory of this day. Do not rush past it — ask follow-ups if she describes the moment in general terms.)*

15. When you sit down at your instruments now, does the false alarm change how you read the data? Are you more cautious, less cautious, or differently cautious?
16. If the same data appeared on your screen tomorrow, what would you do?

---

**HIGH-STAKES — Reckoning and Responsibility** *(Goal: Create space for the subject to confront the tension between scientific uncertainty and the human consequences of her recommendations.)*

17. Do you believe you made the right call six years ago — given what you knew at the time?
18. What does it feel like to know that the next time you issue a warning, people may not listen?
19. If this volcano erupts while you are the person responsible for monitoring it — and people die — what would you need to know you had done?
20. Is there anything about this work that you carry alone — that nobody outside your field understands?

*(Production note: Question 18 is the most critical question in the set. It addresses the central tension of the film: what happens when the person responsible for warning a city has already been wrong once. Give extended silence after this question.)*

---

**Next Step:** If time is short, prioritize questions 12, 17, and 18 — they cover the decision, the reckoning, and the forward-looking fear. Before filming, research the specific seismic data from the false alarm event to be able to ask informed follow-ups. Ensure release forms are signed. Run scene-breakdown-writer to plan the visual coverage — particularly sequences at the monitoring station and any location with a view of the volcano above the city.

## Output 3 (input-03): Minimal input — A teacher

**OPENING — Establishing Voice and World** *(Goal: Let the subject find their voice and establish the texture of their working life before any thematic questions.)*

1. You have been teaching for twenty years. Take me back to the beginning — what made you decide to become a teacher, and how close was that original idea to what the job turned out to be?
2. Describe a typical school day for you — from the moment you arrive to the moment you leave. Not the official version, but the real one.
3. When you tell people you are a teacher, what is the most common thing they say — and what do you wish they would ask instead?

*(Production note: Question 3 often surfaces frustration or pride that the subject has been carrying but has never been asked to articulate. Let it run.)*

---

**SUBSTANTIVE — The Work and What It Costs** *(Goal: Move from the factual to the reflective, building a portrait of what teaching actually requires over two decades.)*

4. What has changed most about teaching since you started — not policy changes or curriculum, but the actual experience of being in a classroom?
5. Describe a moment in the last year when you felt like the job was working — when something happened in the room that reminded you why you do this.
6. Now describe a moment when you seriously questioned whether you could keep doing it.
7. What does a school look like from the inside that it does not look like from the outside? What would surprise someone who has not been in a classroom since they were a student?
8. Who are you closest to in your school — and what does that relationship look like day to day?
9. How do you handle the gap between what you are asked to do and what you have time and resources to actually do?
10. What do you think your students understand about your life — and what do they have no idea about?
11. When you lose a student — not to death, but to disengagement, to dropping out, to something you could not prevent — what does that feel like?
12. How has the job changed you as a person? Not professionally — personally.
13. What is the thing about teaching that takes the most out of you and that nobody talks about?

*(Production note: Questions 11 and 13 are the strongest for emotional, usable material. Both may require follow-up silence.)*

---

**HIGH-STAKES — Reckoning** *(Goal: Create space for honesty about whether the profession is sustainable, valued, or worth recommending.)*

14. If your own child told you they wanted to become a teacher, what would you say — honestly?
15. Twenty years from now, what do you think teaching will look like — and does that version of the job still have a place for someone like you?
16. What do you want people who make decisions about education — politicians, administrators, parents — to understand about what this job actually is?
17. Is there anything about your experience as a teacher that you have never said publicly and that you want on the record?

*(Production note: Question 14 is the most revealing question in the set. The answer almost always tells you whether the subject is still in the profession by choice or by circumstance. Give it space.)*

---

**Next Step:** If time is short, prioritize questions 6, 14, and 16 — they cover the hardest moment, the personal reckoning, and the public statement. Before filming, gather basic context on the school where the subject teaches (size, demographics, any recent news) to ask informed follow-ups. Ensure release forms are signed. Consider running scene-breakdown-writer to plan visual coverage in the classroom and school environment.

*Note: This question set is necessarily broader than a targeted brief would produce. The input provided limited background, so questions are designed to discover the story rather than pursue a specific editorial angle. A more detailed brief — including the specific aspect of education the film is exploring and what the subject is known for — would produce more targeted questions.*

## Output 4 (input-04): European/international context — Italian mayor and migration

**OPENING — Establishing Voice and World** *(Goal: Let the subject settle into their own narrative about the town before we introduce the policy and its politics.)*

1. Describe this town for someone who has never been here. What does it look like, what does it feel like, and what kind of place was it when you were growing up?
2. You became mayor of a town that was losing its population. What was that like in practical terms — what does a town look like when people are leaving?
3. When the school closed, what did that mean for the town — not administratively, but for the families and the community?
4. At what point did you understand that if nothing changed, this town would not survive in any recognisable form?

*(Production note: Question 2 is a world-building question. The answer gives the audience the visual texture of decline — empty houses, closed shops, aging faces — that the film will need for its observational sequences.)*

---

**SUBSTANTIVE — The Policy and Its Consequences** *(Goal: Build the chronology of the decision and its effects, letting the subject narrate rather than defend.)*

5. Take me to the moment you first considered welcoming migrants as a way to reverse the decline. Where did the idea come from?
6. Who supported you when you proposed it, and who opposed it? What were the arguments on each side?
7. Walk me through the first arrivals. How many people came, from where, and what did the first days look like — practically?
8. Describe the moment the school reopened. What was that day like?
9. How do 200 newcomers change the daily rhythm of a town of 2,800 people? What looks different, sounds different, feels different?
10. What has worked better than you expected?
11. What has been harder than you expected?
12. There have been tensions between some residents and the newcomers. Without asking you to describe specific incidents, can you describe the nature of those tensions — what they are about at their root?
13. How do you hold together a community where some people feel the town has been saved and others feel it has been lost?
14. What do the newcomers themselves say about living here? What do they want that they are not getting?

*(Production note: Question 12 approaches the guarded territory indirectly — asking about the nature of tension rather than specific incidents. This gives the subject room to be honest without feeling exposed. Question 14 shifts the perspective away from the mayor's narrative, which is important for balance.)*

15. What do you say to residents who feel that the town they knew is gone — and that this is not what they wanted?

---

**HIGH-STAKES — Reckoning and Legacy** *(Goal: Create space for the subject to confront the costs of the policy alongside its benefits, and to reflect on what they have built.)*

16. Is this what you imagined it would be — or has the reality been different from the version you had in your head when you started?
17. If this policy fails — if the newcomers leave, if the tensions prove too great, if the next mayor reverses everything — what will you feel you achieved?
18. There are hundreds of towns across Southern Europe in the same position yours was. What would you tell another mayor who is considering doing what you did?
19. Twenty years from now, what do you want this town to look like — and who do you want to be living in it?
20. Is there anything about this experience that you have never been asked and that you want on record?

*(Production note: Question 17 is the most important question in the interview. It asks the subject to confront failure as a real possibility, not a hypothetical. Give extended silence.)*

---

**Next Step:** If time is short, prioritize questions 5, 13, and 17 — they cover the origin of the decision, the central tension, and the reckoning with possible failure. Before filming, research the specific demographics and arrival timeline, and speak informally with at least two longtime residents and two newcomers to have context for follow-up questions. Ensure release forms are signed and any translation needs are arranged. Run scene-breakdown-writer to plan visual coverage — particularly the school, any communal spaces where both groups interact, and the physical landscape of the town.

## Output 5 (input-05): Vague/ambiguous input — "Someone important"

**OPENING — Establishing Voice and World** *(Goal: Let the subject settle into who they are and how they see their own life before any thematic questions.)*

1. Tell me about your life right now — what does a normal day look like for you, start to finish?
2. How would you describe what you do to someone who knows nothing about you?
3. When did you first realise that the thing you are known for was going to define a significant part of your life?

*(Production note: Question 3 is a discovery question. Since the brief provides minimal detail, this question is designed to surface the subject's own framing of their significance.)*

---

**SUBSTANTIVE — The Story** *(Goal: These questions are deliberately broad, designed to locate the story rather than pursue it. Use the subject's early answers to guide follow-ups in real time.)*

4. Take me back to the beginning of what happened. Where does the story start, in your telling?
5. Who else was involved — and what role did they play?
6. Walk me through the hardest period. What was happening, and what were you doing about it?
7. What did you think was going to happen — and how was the reality different?
8. What surprised you most about how other people responded to what you did?
9. Describe a specific moment — one you can picture clearly — that captures something essential about this experience.
10. What did you lose in this process that you did not expect to lose?
11. What did you gain that you did not expect to gain?
12. Who understood what you were going through, and who did not?
13. Is there a version of events that the public has that you think is wrong — not wrong in a defamatory way, but just not what actually happened?

*(Production note: Question 9 is the most important question in the substantive section. It forces the subject to move from abstraction to specificity. If they give a general answer, ask them to describe what they could see, hear, and feel. Question 13 is a softener before the high-stakes section.)*

---

**HIGH-STAKES — Reckoning** *(Goal: Even with minimal briefing, these questions create space for honest reflection.)*

14. Knowing everything you know now, what would you do differently?
15. What do you want people to understand about this that they currently do not?
16. What has this cost you — personally, not professionally?
17. Is there anything you have never been asked about this, and that you want said?

*(Production note: These questions are generic by necessity. The brief provided minimal subject detail, so this question set is designed to locate the story rather than tell it. A more detailed brief — including who the subject is, what they did, and what the film is specifically exploring — would produce significantly more targeted and effective questions.)*

---

**Next Step:** If time is short, prioritize questions 6, 9, and 14 — they cover the hardest period, the most specific moment, and the reckoning. Before filming, gather substantially more background on the subject: who they are, what they did, what the film is specifically about, and what they are likely to be guarded about. Without this information, the interviewer will need to do significant real-time adaptation. Ensure release forms are signed. Consider running scene-breakdown-writer only after the interview brief is more fully developed.

*Note: This question set reflects the limited input provided. The interview-question-generator produces its strongest output when given specific details about the subject, the editorial angle, and what the subject is likely to be guarded about. A revised brief with these details would produce a substantially more targeted question set.*
